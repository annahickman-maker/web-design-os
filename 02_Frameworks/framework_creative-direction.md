# Creative Direction Framework

Creative direction is the visual translation of the brand strategy. It turns the abstract strategy into a concrete aesthetic that the designer can build from.

---

## What creative direction includes

### 1. Visual Mood
The overall feeling the brand should evoke. Captured through:
- Moodboard (curated reference imagery)
- Descriptive language (e.g. "premium brand book meets editorial magazine")
- Aesthetic references (e.g. "inspired by Japanese minimalism and Scandinavian craft")

### 2. Color Palette
A specific set of colors that define the brand's visual identity:
- **Primary** - the signature color
- **Secondary** - supporting colors (usually 2-3)
- **Neutrals** - backgrounds and text (off-whites, greys, blacks)
- **Dark mode variants** (optional but recommended)

Rules:
- Limit to 3-5 meaningful colors
- Ensure accessibility contrast ratios (WCAG AA minimum)
- Consider both light and dark mode
- Avoid generic "startup" palettes unless the brand is a startup

### 3. Typography
Font choices that carry the brand's personality:
- **Display / Heading font** - used for H1, H2, major callouts
- **Body font** - used for paragraphs, UI, everything else
- **Optional accent font** - for special moments (quotes, badges)

Rules:
- Rarely more than 2 font families
- Display font should have character; body font should be highly legible
- Consider mood alignment (serif = traditional/editorial, sans = modern/clean, mono = technical)

### 4. Photography Style
The type of imagery the brand will use:
- **Subject matter** - people, products, environments, abstract
- **Style** - editorial, lifestyle, documentary, commercial, clinical
- **Treatment** - color grading, lighting, composition
- **Source** - custom shoots vs curated stock

Rules:
- Photography often does the heaviest visual work - choose carefully
- Stock photography can work if curated with taste; avoid obvious stock clichés
- Consistency matters more than polish

### 5. Design Style Mockups
Example website elements showing the creative direction applied:
- Sample heading styles
- CTA button designs
- Card/component styles
- Hero section examples

These give the client a concrete preview of what their website will feel like.

---

## Moodboard curation

The moodboard is the single most important creative direction deliverable. Rules for building one:

### Do
- Curate 6-12 images that share a cohesive feeling
- Include variety (photography, typography, UI, texture, color)
- Annotate why each image is relevant
- Include actual website screenshots as references
- Consider what the brand ISN'T as much as what it IS

### Don't
- Grab random images that "look nice"
- Include contradictory styles
- Rely only on Pinterest defaults
- Show 40 images (no one can process that)
- Include your own past work unless it's genuinely relevant

---

## The moodboard serves the strategy

The creative direction should always trace back to:

1. **Brand identity** - Does this feel like the values and mission?
2. **Target audience** - Would the personas recognise themselves here?
3. **Positioning** - Does this differentiate from competitors?
4. **Customer journey** - Will this work at each stage they go through?

If a moodboard looks beautiful but doesn't serve the strategy, it's wrong.

---

## Where the creative direction comes from

In the new AI-powered workflow, creative direction is partially auto-generated and partially human:

### Auto-generated by AI:
- Descriptive mood language based on brand identity and tone of voice
- Suggested aesthetic themes
- Starting color palette suggestions based on brand values
- Typography recommendations based on tone

### Curated by the designer (human required):
- Actual moodboard image selection
- Final color palette decisions
- Final typography choices
- Custom design mockups

AI can suggest directions, but the designer makes the taste decisions. This is where designer skill matters most.

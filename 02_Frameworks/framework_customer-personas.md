# Customer Personas Framework

Customer personas are the bridge between audience research and messaging. They make the abstract "target audience" concrete enough that the designer can write copy as if speaking to a specific person.

---

## Why personas matter

Generic audience definitions like "small business owners" or "fitness enthusiasts" don't produce strong copy. Personas force specificity. When the designer writes homepage copy for "Marcus, 34, a corporate escapee building his first coaching business, drowning in Canva templates and generic advice," the copy sounds like a conversation. When they write for "entrepreneurs", it sounds like marketing.

---

## Persona Structure

Each persona has 3 components:

### 1. Name (memorable label)
Not the person's actual name - a short descriptor that captures the essence. The examples from the strategy deck reference use names like:

- "The Obsessed"
- "The Explorer"
- "The Overwhelmed Founder"
- "The Accidental CEO"
- "The Career Pivot"

The name should be a descriptor the audience would recognise themselves in without being insulting.

### 2. Description (3 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1: Who they are and what drives them**
- Their current situation
- What motivates them
- What they care about
- What they value

**Paragraph 2: Where they are in their journey**
- Their experience level
- What they've already tried
- What hasn't worked
- Where they're stuck

**Paragraph 3: What they're looking for in a brand**
- The type of relationship they want
- What signals they trust
- What turns them off
- What would make them say yes

### 3. Visual reference (optional)
A photo that captures the persona's vibe. Not a stock photo of a generic "business person" - a cinematic, specific image that feels real. The strategy deck uses outdoor, editorial-style photography.

---

## How many personas?

2 personas per brand is the standard. Two provides enough range without creating a mess of conflicting messaging.

The two personas should represent meaningfully different segments. Examples:

- **The Obsessed** (deeply committed fitness enthusiast) + **The Explorer** (new to fitness, just starting)
- **The Corporate Escapee** (high-income, leaving their 9-5) + **The Side Hustler** (building alongside their job)
- **The First-Timer** (never had a website) + **The Rebuilder** (had a bad website and wants something better)

One persona is usually the "ideal" higher-value customer. The other is the entry-level customer the brand can grow with.

## Example personas

### The Obsessed

Individuals who are deeply committed to their fitness journey and are seeking more than just physical gains.

They are motivated by the idea of holistic well-being, embracing both physical strength and mental resilience.

They have experience in various forms of exercise and are now searching for a brand that aligns with their values of sustainable living, community support, and continuous self-improvement.

### The Explorer

Those who are new to the world of fitness and wellness but are eager to embark on a transformative journey.

They may have tried sporadic workouts in the past or are simply starting their exploration of healthy living.

This audience is looking for a brand that provides approachable guidance, personalized support, and a sense of belonging to a community that encourages and celebrates every step of their progress.

---

## Writing rules for personas

1. **Use specific language the customer would use themselves** - not marketing terms. "Stuck at $2k months" not "facing revenue challenges."
2. **Reference real situations** - "opens Canva, closes Canva, opens Instagram" captures frustration better than "struggles with tools."
3. **Show the internal monologue** - what do they tell themselves? What do they complain about to friends?
4. **Avoid pity** - personas should feel like real people the reader respects, not victims the brand will save.
5. **Make them aspirational but relatable** - the reader should think "that's me, and I want to be the version of me who's figured this out."

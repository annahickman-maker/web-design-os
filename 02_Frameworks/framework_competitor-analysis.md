# Competitor Analysis Framework

Competitor analysis positions the brand in the market. Done well, it reveals gaps the brand can occupy. Done badly, it becomes a copycat exercise. The goal is always to find what the competitors aren't doing and claim that space.

---

## Structure

For each competitor, capture four dimensions:

### 1. USP (Unique Selling Proposition)
What the competitor is known for. Their main positioning claim. One to two sentences.

### 2. Strengths
What they do well. Market position, reputation, product quality, content, community, pricing, operations.

### 3. Weaknesses
Where they fall short. Gaps in their offer, weak messaging, poor customer experience, outdated positioning, missing segments.

### 4. How We're Different
The most important column. Based on their USP, strengths, and weaknesses - what is the opening for our brand? This is where positioning gets built.

---

## How many competitors?

3 is the standard. Fewer than 3 feels thin. More than 3 becomes noise.

Choose:
- **1 market leader** - the obvious big name everyone mentions
- **1 direct competitor** - someone occupying similar space at similar scale
- **1 adjacent competitor** - someone in a related space who might steal the same customers

---

## Research sources

When running automated competitor analysis:

1. **Visit their website** - pull positioning, services, pricing, about page
2. **Read their about page** - understand their story and values
3. **Check their testimonials** - see how customers describe them
4. **Review their social media** - tone, frequency, engagement
5. **Read reviews** - Trustpilot, Google, industry-specific platforms
6. **Check their blog/content** - what topics do they own, what do they avoid

---

## Writing rules

### For strengths
- Be specific, not generic. "Strong global brand recognition, extensive product range, athlete endorsements" not "good marketing."
- Name what they actually do well. This is observation, not flattery.

### For weaknesses
- Focus on gaps, not flaws. "Premium pricing that excludes casual customers" is more useful than "too expensive."
- Look for things they don't talk about, not just things they do badly.
- Consider their implicit assumptions - what do they take for granted that the brand could challenge?

### For "How We're Different"
- Should feel like a strategic opportunity, not a random difference
- Must connect to what the target audience actually wants
- Should be defensible - the competitor shouldn't be able to easily copy it
- Should be something the brand is genuinely prepared to commit to

---

## Example

### Competitor: Nike
- **USP:** Innovative athletic footwear and apparel, emphasising cutting-edge technology and performance enhancement.
- **Strengths:** Strong global brand recognition, extensive product range, athlete endorsements, emphasis on research and development, iconic "swoosh" logo.
- **Weaknesses:** Premium pricing, intense competition, occasional controversies related to labor practices or marketing campaigns.
- **How We're Different:** Focus on sustainability and ethical practices in manufacturing, promoting inclusivity for all fitness levels, and creating a community-driven approach to fitness.

---

## Turning the analysis into positioning

After the analysis, the system generates two outputs:

### USP Statement
A one-sentence statement of the brand's unique position in the market. Based on the "How We're Different" column across all competitors.

Example: "Prioritising functional and performance-oriented activewear that seamlessly blends fashion and fitness, with a focus on showing a range of different approaches to a healthy lifestyle outside of a traditional gym environment."

### Messaging Statement
The core message that carries across the brand. Not a tagline - the underlying idea.

Example: "We are shaping a healthier, more vibrant world, one workout, one connection, one conscious choice at a time."

These two statements feed directly into the strategy deck's "How We Set Ourselves Apart" page.
